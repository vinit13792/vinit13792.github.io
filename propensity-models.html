What It Is

A production machine learning system that predicts the probability of a customer investing in Fixed Deposits, enabling precision targeting that transformed a ₹15 Crore marketing budget into ₹520 Crores of incremental deposit mobilization. This wasn't merely a classification problem—it was a fundamental reimagining of how a ₹2.2 Lakh Crore AUC Non-Banking Financial Company approaches customer engagement.

The system comprises three interconnected models: a propensity scorer that ranks 5.2 million customers by their likelihood to convert, a survival analysis model that predicts optimal contact timing, and a Bayesian Market Mix Model that attributes conversions across channels and optimizes budget allocation. Together, these models increased campaign conversion rates from 0.8% to 3.4% while reducing customer acquisition costs by 78%.

But the numbers only tell part of the story. The real challenge wasn't building models—it was building models that worked in production, models that sales teams trusted, models that could be explained to a chief risk officer who'd spent three decades in traditional banking. This is the story of technical decisions made under business pressure, of trade-offs between statistical elegance and operational reality, and of the painful lessons that only emerge when machine learning meets mass-market financial services.

The Problem: A Perfect Storm

Shriram Finance sits at a unique position in India's financial landscape. As one of the largest retail NBFCs, the company has built its business on serving customers that traditional banks overlook—small business owners, self-employed professionals, and individuals with thin credit files. This customer base of 5.2 million is both a strength and a vulnerability. They're loyal, but they're also price-sensitive and financially sophisticated enough to chase 25 basis points across institutions.

In Q4 FY2024, the treasury team flagged a ₹5,000 Crore funding gap projected for the next fiscal year. Fixed Deposits, accounting for 42% of total borrowing, needed to accelerate. The existing marketing approach—broadcast SMS campaigns, generic email newsletters, and branch-level outreach—was achieving 0.8% conversion. At that rate, filling the gap would require either doubling the marketing budget or accepting 18 months of aggressive campaigning.

The Data Reality Check

Our first audit revealed the depth of the challenge. The customer data ecosystem had evolved organically over 15 years, accumulating technical debt at every acquisition and system migration:

Pain Point #1: Fragmented Identity

A single customer could exist as 3 different entities: CUST_001 in the legacy loan management system, FD_84729 in the deposit portal, and +91-98XXX in the campaign management platform. Identity resolution required fuzzy matching across 7 databases with no canonical source of truth.

Pain Point #2: Missing Value Predicament

Income data was missing for 34% of customers—those who'd opened FDs directly through digital channels without submitting income proof. Employment type was unpopulated for customers acquired before 2018's KYC mandate. The missingness wasn't random: higher-net-worth customers were more likely to have missing employment data due to expedited onboarding processes.

Pain Point #3: Temporal Misalignment

Campaign response data existed, but the timestamps were stored in three different time zones (IST, UTC, and system local time for a legacy server in Chennai). Response attribution required reconstructing the actual customer journey from fragmented logs.

Pain Point #4: Extreme Class Imbalance

Of 5.2 million customers, only 42,000 had invested in an FD in the preceding 12 months—a positive rate of 0.8%. Standard resampling techniques would either undersample the majority class into oblivion or oversample the minority class into memorization territory.

The Business Constraints

Technical debt was only half the problem. The business constraints formed an equally formidable barrier:

  • Budget Ceiling: ₹15 Crores total marketing budget for the fiscal year. Every rupee needed justification.
  • Competitive Pressure: HDFC Bank had launched a 7.25% FD rate. Shriram's 6.75% offering required smarter targeting to compete.
  • Regulatory Scrutiny: RBI's Fair Practices Code prohibited discriminatory targeting. Model decisions needed audit trails.
  • Operational Capacity: Branch staff could make 800 calls per day. We needed to surface the right 800 customers.
  • Channel Constraints: WhatsApp Business API costs ₹0.33 per conversation. Spamming 5 million customers wasn't economically viable.

The CRO's mandate crystallized into a deceptively simple question: "Build me a system that tells me exactly who to call, when to call, and what to offer." Elegantly stated. Brutally difficult to execute.

Mathematical Formulation

Before diving into implementation, we formalized the problem mathematically. This wasn't academic exercise—precise formulation forced clarity on what we were actually optimizing for.

The Objective Function

Given a customer c with feature vector x_c, we seek to maximize expected return:

max Σ_c [ P(conversion | x_c) × Revenue_c - Cost(contact_c) ] × I(contact_c)

Where I(contact_c) is an indicator function: 1 if we decide to contact customer c, 0 otherwise. The constraint is the budget:

Σ_c Cost(contact_c) × I(contact_c) ≤ Budget

This formulation reveals an immediate insight: we're not trying to predict who will convert. We're trying to predict who will convert if contacted. These are fundamentally different questions, and conflating them leads to targeting customers who would have converted anyway—the classic "preaching to the choir" problem.

The Causal Challenge

Standard supervised learning estimates P(conversion | features) from observational data. But this conflates two quantities:

P(conversion | features) = P(conversion | contacted, features) × P(contacted | features) + P(conversion | not contacted, features) × P(not contacted | features)

The second term—the probability of conversion without contact—represents organic conversions that would have happened regardless. In financial services, this fraction can be substantial: customers with maturing FDs, those seeking tax-advantaged instruments before fiscal year-end, those responding to competitive pressure. Targeting these customers yields no incremental value; they would have converted anyway.

We needed to estimate uplift—the causal effect of contact:

Uplift_c = P(conversion | contacted, x_c) - P(conversion | not contacted, x_c)
Solution: Two-Model Approach

We trained separate models: one on contacted customers, another on a holdout sample. The difference in predicted probabilities approximated uplift. This required sacrificing 15% of our campaign budget to maintain a randomized holdout—a difficult sell to business stakeholders, but essential for causal inference.

Feature Engineering: The Real Work

In applied machine learning, feature engineering often determines success more than model architecture. We invested 8 of our 12-week timeline on feature engineering alone. The result: 247 features across 7 categories, each with a hypothesis for why it might predict FD propensity.

Category 1: RFM Features

Recency, Frequency, Monetary—the workhorse features of customer lifetime value modeling. But in the FD context, these required careful interpretation:

  • Recency: Days since last FD investment. A customer who invested 6 months ago might have deployable capital; one who invested yesterday likely doesn't.
  • Frequency: Number of FD investments in past 24 months. High frequency indicates both trust and habit—but also signals a customer who actively manages finances and might be rate-shopping.
  • Monetary: Total FD volume. Critical for segmentation, but past investment volume doesn't guarantee future capacity.

Category 2: Liquidity Score (Proprietary)

Our most predictive feature emerged from domain knowledge, not raw data. The Liquidity Score captures deployable surplus—money available for FD investment. We derived it from:

L_c = (SavingsBalance_c + FDMaturityProceeds_90d_c - EMI_Obligations_c) / MonthlyIncome_c

The numerator represents money the customer could invest. The denominator normalizes for income level. A customer with ₹2 Lakhs in savings and ₹50,000 monthly obligations has higher deployable capacity than one with ₹5 Lakhs savings but ₹4.5 Lakhs in obligations.

The 90-day FD maturity window proved critical. Customers with upcoming FD maturities face a decision point: renew with the current institution, or shop around. Contacting them 7-14 days before maturity—with a pre-approved renewal offer—captured conversions before competitors could intervene.

Category 3: Behavioral Features

How customers interact with communications reveals intent. We engineered:

EngagementScore_c = α × EmailOpenRate_c + β × SMSClickRate_c + γ × WhatsAppReadRate_c

Where α, β, γ are channel-specific weights learned from held-out validation data. A customer who opens every email but never clicks has different intent than one who clicks immediately. Peak engagement hour (the time of day a customer most frequently opens communications) informed when to contact, not just whether.

Category 4: Temporal Features

Investment behavior isn't stationary. We captured dynamics:

FDVelocity_c = FDCount_12m_c / max(FDCount_24m_c, 1)

Velocity > 1 indicates accelerating investment frequency. Velocity < 1 signals deceleration—perhaps deteriorating finances or competitive churn. Both conditions merit different treatment.

The Missing Value Problem: Solutions and Trade-offs

Feature engineering revealed the missing value problem in full force. We evaluated multiple approaches:

Approach 1: Deletion

Remove records with missing values. Result: Lost 34% of customers. Unacceptable.

Approach 2: Mean Imputation

Fill missing income with mean income. Result: Systematic underestimation for high-net-worth customers, overestimation for others.

Approach 3: MICE

Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations. Result: Computationally expensive at 5.2M scale; convergence issues with MNAR patterns.

Chosen: Native Handling + Flag Features

XGBoost's native missing value handling + binary flags indicating missingness. The income_missing feature itself became predictive—an indicator of digital-native customers who bypassed traditional onboarding.

Model Development: Decisions Under Pressure

With features engineered, we faced the model selection decision. This wasn't a Kaggle competition where the best AUC wins. This was production deployment where interpretability, latency, and maintenance burden mattered equally with accuracy.

Algorithm Comparison

We benchmarked four algorithms on held-out data:

Metric Logistic Reg Random Forest XGBoost LightGBM
AUC-ROC 0.891 0.923 0.947 0.944
AUC-PR 0.287 0.356 0.412 0.408
Lift @ 10% 18.2x 24.7x 29.3x 28.9x
Training Time 2 min 45 min 12 min 8 min
Inference Latency 2ms 15ms 12ms 9ms

XGBoost won on accuracy metrics, LightGBM on speed. The deciding factor? SHAP interpretability. XGBoost's mature SHAP implementation allowed us to explain individual predictions—to answer the question "Why did this customer score 0.73?" with precise feature contributions.

The Class Imbalance Challenge

With 0.8% positive rate, standard cross-entropy loss was hopeless. The model would learn to always predict negative and achieve 99.2% accuracy—useless for our purposes. We evaluated three strategies:

Class Weight: scale_pos_weight = N_negative / N_positive = 124

Setting scale_pos_weight=124 told XGBoost to treat false negatives as 124× more costly than false positives. This shifted the decision boundary but introduced calibration issues—the raw probability scores no longer reflected true probabilities.

We also experimented with SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique), generating synthetic positive examples. The results were disappointing: on validation data, SMOTE models exhibited severe overfitting, with AUC-PR actually decreasing compared to class-weighted training. The synthetic examples, interpolated from real positives, introduced artifacts the model memorized.

Final Approach: Class Weights + Threshold Optimization

We trained with scale_pos_weight=124, accepted miscalibrated probabilities, and then optimized the decision threshold on validation data using a business cost function rather than statistical metrics.

Threshold Optimization: Where Statistics Meets Business

The default threshold of 0.5 is rarely optimal in imbalanced settings. We optimized threshold using a custom business cost function:

Cost(TP) = +₹50,000 (average FD ticket revenue)
Cost(FP) = -₹150 (marketing cost for non-converter)
Cost(FN) = -₹50,000 (opportunity cost of missed conversion)
Cost(TN) = ₹0

Scanning thresholds from 0.1 to 0.9, we computed expected profit:

ExpectedProfit(t) = TP(t) × 50000 - FP(t) × 150 - FN(t) × 50000

The optimal threshold: 0.34. Not the threshold maximizing F1 score (which was 0.47), not the threshold maximizing Youden's J statistic (0.41), but the threshold that maximized business value. This gap between statistical and business optimality is often overlooked—and costly.

Market Mix Modelling: The Attribution Problem

Propensity scores told us who to target. Market Mix Modelling (MMM) answered a different question: where should we allocate budget across channels?

This is fundamentally a causal inference problem. We observe conversions and channel exposures. We want to know: if we had spent more on WhatsApp and less on SMS, how would conversions have changed?

The Structural Model

We specified a Bayesian structural model incorporating two key phenomena:

1. Adstock (Carryover Effect): Advertising effects persist beyond the exposure period. An SMS sent today influences decisions for weeks.

Adstock_t = Spend_t + θ × Spend_(t-1) + θ² × Spend_(t-2) + ...

The decay parameter θ captures how quickly advertising effects fade. For SMS, we expected rapid decay (customers act quickly or not at all). For email, slower decay (messages sit in inboxes for days).

2. Saturation (Diminishing Returns): The first ₹1 Lakh spent on a channel has more impact than the 10th ₹1 Lakh.

Saturation(x) = x^α / (x^α + K^α)

K is the half-saturation point—spend level achieving 50% of maximum possible effect. α controls steepness: higher α means sharper saturation.

The Full Model Specification

Conversions_t ~ Normal(μ_t, σ)

μ_t = α + Σᵢ βᵢ × Hill(Adstock(Spendᵢ,t , θᵢ), Kᵢ, αᵢ)

Where i ∈ {SMS, Email, WhatsApp, Branch, Digital}

We fit this model using PyMC, running 4 chains with 2000 samples each. Convergence diagnostics showed R-hat < 1.01 for all parameters, but trace plots revealed slow mixing—synthetic data tests suggested we needed 6+ weeks of data for reliable inference. With only 12 weeks of clean campaign data, uncertainty in channel coefficients was substantial.

What We Learned

Despite uncertainty, the posterior distributions revealed clear patterns:

  • WhatsApp: β̂ = 0.42 [0.31, 0.58] 95% CI. Highest ROI, fastest-growing channel.
  • Branch Calls: β̂ = 0.38 [0.29, 0.47]. Tried and true—high conversion for contacted customers, but operational bottleneck.
  • SMS: β̂ = 0.18 [0.11, 0.26]. Low cost, but also low impact. Volume without targeting.
  • Email: β̂ = 0.11 [0.06, 0.17]. Lowest ROI. Many customers don't read marketing emails.

The recommendation: reallocate from SMS to WhatsApp, maintain Branch investment, reduce Email. Conservative application (shifting 20% of budget) rather than aggressive reallocation—MMM uncertainty warranted humility.

Production Challenges: When Models Meet Reality

Challenge 1: Feature Drift

Three weeks after deployment, we noticed declining model performance. Investigation revealed the culprit: a new campaign had offered preferential FD rates to customers with FD maturity in the next 30 days. This created a new, strong signal—fd_maturity_window feature—which the model had never seen at training time.

The feature's distribution shifted:

P(fd_maturity_window = 1) increased from 3.2% to 8.7%

Model predictions for this segment became unreliable—not because the model was wrong, but because the world had changed. We implemented drift monitoring using Population Stability Index (PSI):

PSI = Σ (Actual_i - Expected_i) × ln(Actual_i / Expected_i)

PSI > 0.2 triggers automatic retraining pipeline. PSI > 0.1 triggers alerts for investigation.

Challenge 2: Cold Start for New Customers

The model required 90 days of behavioral history. New customers scored near-random probabilities. Our solution: a separate "cold start" model trained on demographic and initial transaction features only. Accuracy was lower (AUC 0.72 vs 0.95), but better than random for the 8% of customers who were new each month.

Challenge 3: Explainability at Scale

Branch managers demanded explanations for scores. "Why is Mrs. Sharma only Tier 3?" Using SHAP, we generated individualized explanations:

Mrs. Sharma's Score: 0.31 (Tier 3)

Factors reducing score:
• Low liquidity score: -0.18 (recent FD maturity redeployed elsewhere)
• Low engagement: -0.12 (no email opens in 90 days)
• Competitive exposure: -0.08 (visited competitor website based on attribution data)

Factors increasing score:
• Prior FD history: +0.15 (2 previous FDs)
• Age (55+): +0.08 (FDs preferred over equity)

Recommendation: Wait 14 days, re-engage via WhatsApp, offer 10 bps rate enhancement.

Challenge 4: The Ethics of Prediction

Predictive models can encode and amplify biases. We conducted fairness audits across age, gender, and geography:

  • Age: Older customers (55+) received systematically higher scores. Was this bias or legitimate signal? Investigation confirmed age correlated with FD preference (risk aversion increases with age). But we added guardrails: no customer could be excluded from campaigns based solely on age.
  • Geography: Rural customers scored lower due to thinner digital footprints. We adjusted behavioral feature weights to reduce geographic disparity.
  • Gender: No significant disparity after controlling for income and occupation.

These audits weren't one-time exercises—they became quarterly reviews with legal and compliance stakeholders.

Incremental Attribution

The ₹520 Crore figure wasn't total FD mobilization—it was incremental contribution, the difference between model-driven targeting and the baseline approach. We measured this through:

  • Holdout analysis: 5% of customers held out from model-driven campaigns continued receiving random outreach. Their conversion rate (0.9%) established counterfactual.
  • Pre/post comparison: Year-over-year FD mobilization growth accelerated from 12% to 24% after model deployment, controlling for market rate changes.
  • Channel-specific attribution: MMM attributed 60% of incremental conversions to improved targeting (propensity scores), 40% to improved channel allocation.

Operational Transformation

Beyond the headline numbers, the system changed how marketing operated:

  • From broadcast to precision: Campaigns now targeted 200,000 high-propensity customers instead of 5 million. Same conversion volume, 25x fewer messages.
  • From reactive to proactive: FD maturity predictions enabled preemptive outreach. Conversion rate for customers contacted 14 days before maturity: 18.2%.
  • From intuition to evidence: Feature importance rankings became boardroom talking points. "Liquidity score explains 18.7% of variance" resonated more than "the model is accurate."

Key Lessons: What I'd Do Differently

Lesson #1: Holdout Groups Are Non-Negotiable

The business resisted sacrificing 15% of campaign budget for randomized holdout. We compromised at 5%. In hindsight, we needed more. Without proper counterfactuals, we couldn't measure true incremental lift until month 4. The statistical rigor should have been defended more forcefully upfront.
Lesson #2: Calibration Matters for Threshold Optimization

We optimized thresholds for business value, but the class-weighted model produced miscalibrated probabilities. This caused issues downstream when other teams tried to use raw scores for risk aggregation. Next time: probability calibration (Platt scaling or isotonic regression) post-training, before threshold optimization.
Lesson #3: Model Monitoring Should Be Built-In, Not Bolted On

We implemented drift monitoring reactively, after performance degraded. It should have been part of the initial deployment—PSI calculations, feature distribution tracking, and automated retraining triggers from day one.
What Went Right

Feature engineering investment: The 8 weeks spent on features paid dividends. The Liquidity Score alone was worth more than any hyperparameter tuning.

Segmentation over prediction: The tier system (Hot/Warm/Cold/Dormant) was more actionable than raw probabilities. Branch staff could operationalize "focus on Tier 1" but struggled with "call everyone above 0.67."

Business partnership: Having a marketing analyst embedded in the data science team accelerated iteration. Features were validated against domain expertise before model training.

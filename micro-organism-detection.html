What It Is

A production computer vision system that detects, counts, and calculates growth rates of micro-organisms in petri dishes. This replaced a ₹1,00,000 proprietary hardware solution with a software-based approach that achieved higher accuracy (94% vs 80%) while eliminating recurring calibration costs. The system was deployed at HiMedia Laboratories, one of India's largest manufacturers of microbiological culture media, processing over 500 samples daily across 5 laboratory locations.

The core challenge in microbiological quality control is quantifying colony-forming units (CFUs) in agar plates. Traditional methods rely on manual counting under magnification or expensive automated colony counters. Manual counting is subjective, time-consuming, and prone to human error—particularly during extended counting sessions. Commercial colony counters automate this process but come with significant capital investment and ongoing maintenance costs. Our solution bridged this gap by leveraging commodity hardware (Raspberry Pi with a high-resolution camera) combined with state-of-the-art instance segmentation models.

The system identifies 15 different micro-organism species commonly used in pharmaceutical and food safety testing, including Escherichia coli, Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Salmonella typhimurium, Candida albicans, and various Bacillus and Aspergillus species. Beyond simple enumeration, the pipeline computes exponential growth rates from time-series imagery, enabling predictive quality metrics and early anomaly detection in culture media performance.

The Problem

HiMedia Laboratories produces over 3,000 distinct culture media formulations for clinical diagnostics, pharmaceutical quality control, and food safety testing. Each batch requires sterility testing and growth promotion testing (GPT) to ensure compliance with pharmacopeial standards (USP, EP, JP). The existing quality control infrastructure had accumulated significant technical debt over the years, creating bottlenecks that constrained production capacity.

Existing Infrastructure Analysis

The laboratory had standardized on a commercial colony counter—a purpose-built instrument combining LED illumination, a magnifying lens, and proprietary image processing software. While this solution worked adequately for standard plate counts, it exhibited several critical limitations that impacted operational efficiency:

Capital Expenditure Constraints

Each colony counter unit cost ₹1,00,000 (approximately $1,200 USD at the time), with annual maintenance contracts adding another ₹15,000-20,000 per unit. For a facility operating 5 production lines with redundant QC stations, the capital outlay exceeded ₹12,00,000 including maintenance reserve. More problematically, the instruments had a mean time between failures (MTBF) of approximately 18 months, with repair turnaround times of 2-3 weeks—during which manual counting protocols had to be activated, creating throughput constraints.

Accuracy Limitations Under Real-World Conditions

The vendor-specified accuracy of 95% was achieved only under ideal conditions: uniform agar depth, optimal lighting, and well-separated colonies. In actual production environments, accuracy degraded significantly. We conducted a systematic audit over 8 weeks, comparing instrument counts against validated manual counts performed by senior microbiologists. The results revealed:

  • Overall accuracy: 78.3% across all sample types (vs. claimed 95%)
  • Edge effects: 23% of colonies near petri dish boundaries were either missed or double-counted
  • Confluence artifacts: At colony densities >150 CFU/plate, accuracy dropped to 61% due to merging colony signals
  • Species-specific variance: Accuracy ranged from 70% (fungal colonies with diffuse edges) to 89% (well-defined bacterial colonies)
  • Color sensitivity: Certain chromogenic media selections caused detection failures when colony color matched agar background

Integration Gaps

The proprietary software operated in isolation—data export required manual CSV file transfers, creating potential for transcription errors. The instrument's SDK was closed-source and vendor support was unresponsive to integration requests. The manufacturing execution system (MES) and laboratory information management system (LIMS) operated independently, requiring technicians to manually transcribe results—a process averaging 4.7 minutes per sample and introducing a 2.1% error rate in batch record entries.

Species Coverage

The instrument came pre-programmed with 5 organism templates: E. coli, S. aureus, P. aeruginosa, B. subtilis, and C. albicans. HiMedia's product portfolio required testing against 15+ organisms for GPT compliance. The remaining species required manual identification and counting—creating a hybrid workflow that negated much of the automation benefit. Vendor quotations for adding organism templates ranged from ₹25,000-40,000 per species with 6-8 week implementation timelines.

Calibration Drift

The LED illumination modules exhibited gradual intensity decay over time, causing systematic undercounting as threshold parameters became mismatched to actual image characteristics. Monthly calibration cycles required production hold periods averaging 2 days per instrument—during which QC throughput dropped by 40% as backup manual counting procedures activated.

The Business Case for Replacement

With production volumes projected to increase 35% over the following fiscal year, the QC bottleneck threatened to become a rate-limiting constraint. We modeled three scenarios: (1) continue with existing instruments and add labor capacity, (2) purchase additional commercial units, (3) develop a custom software solution. The total cost of ownership (TCO) analysis over 5 years favored option 3 by a margin of ₹18,00,000 when accounting for hardware, maintenance, calibration downtime, and labor costs.

How It Works

The system architecture follows a modular pipeline design, enabling independent optimization of each processing stage. This separation of concerns proved invaluable during iterative development—we could modify the detection model without touching image acquisition, and vice versa. The complete pipeline processes a single plate in approximately 3.2 seconds on the target hardware (NVIDIA T4 GPU instance), with the image acquisition stage being the rate-limiting step at 2.1 seconds.

Stage 1: Image Acquisition Pipeline

Image acquisition is the foundation of any computer vision system—any artifacts introduced at this stage propagate through the entire pipeline, potentially causing catastrophic detection failures. We designed a custom imaging rig using off-the-shelf components to ensure reproducibility and minimize maintenance complexity.

Hardware Configuration

  • Compute Module: Raspberry Pi 4 Model B (4GB RAM)
  • Image Sensor: Sony IMX477 (12.3 MP, 1/2.3" sensor, 1.55μm pixel pitch)
  • Lens: 16mm telephoto C-mount lens with adjustable iris
  • Illumination: Custom LED ring light (5600K, 92 CRI), diffused through opal acrylic
  • Enclosure: 3D-printed light-tight housing with magnetic lid for sample access

The choice of Sony IMX477 over cheaper sensors was deliberate—the sensor's high quantum efficiency (QE) in the visible spectrum and excellent signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) at base ISO enabled clean captures without aggressive gain amplification. The 1/2.3" sensor size with 12MP resolution provided adequate spatial sampling: at our working distance of 25cm and 16mm focal length, each pixel covered approximately 18μm on the sample plane—sufficient to resolve colony boundaries as small as 0.5mm diameter.

Illumination Engineering

Illumination uniformity is critical for reproducible segmentation. We conducted a series of calibration tests using a matte white reference target (Spectralon 99% reflectance standard) and measured intensity variation across the field of view (FOV). Initial designs using a single LED array produced intensity fall-off of 23% from center to edge (cos⁴θ vignetting). The final ring light configuration with double-diffusion reduced this to 4.7% variation—within acceptable tolerance for the detection pipeline.

Illumination Uniformity = (Imax - Imin) / Iavg × 100% < 5% (target specification)

Capture Automation

import picamera
import picamera.array
import time
import json
from datetime import datetime
import exiftool

class ColonyImager:
    """
    Automated petri dish imaging system with quality validation.

    Handles camera initialization, capture triggering, and metadata
    extraction for downstream processing pipeline integration.
    """

    def __init__(self, config_path='config/imaging.json'):
        with open(config_path) as f:
            self.config = json.load(f)

        self.camera = picamera.PiCamera()
        self._configure_camera()

    def _configure_camera(self):
        """Apply validated imaging parameters from config."""
        self.camera.resolution = tuple(self.config['resolution'])
        self.camera.iso = self.config['iso']
        self.camera.shutter_speed = self.config['shutter_speed']
        self.camera.exposure_mode = 'off'  # Lock exposure
        self.camera.awb_mode = 'tungsten'  # Match LED color temp
        self.camera.awb_gains = (1.2, 1.4)  # Fine-tuned for 5600K LEDs

    def capture_with_validation(self, sample_id, output_dir='samples/'):
        """
        Capture image with automated quality checks.

        Returns path to validated image file or raises exception
        if quality thresholds not met.
        """
        filepath = f"{output_dir}{sample_id}.jpg"

        # Warm-up and auto-adjust
        self.camera.start_preview()
        time.sleep(self.config['stabilization_delay'])

        # Capture with EXIF metadata
        self.camera.capture(
            filepath,
            format='jpeg',
            quality=95,
            exif=self._generate_exif_tags(sample_id)
        )
        self.camera.stop_preview()

        # Quality validation
        if not self._validate_capture(filepath):
            raise ImageQualityError(f"Capture failed validation: {filepath}")

        return filepath

    def _validate_capture(self, filepath):
        """
        Verify image meets quality thresholds.

        Checks: blur detection, exposure range, color balance.
        Returns True if all checks pass.
        """
        import cv2
        import numpy as np

        img = cv2.imread(filepath)
        gray = cv2.cvtColor(img, cv2.COLOR_BGR2GRAY)

        # Blur detection using Laplacian variance
        laplacian_var = cv2.Laplacian(gray, cv2.CV_64F).var()
        if laplacian_var < self.config['min_laplace_variance']:
            print(f"Blur detected: variance={laplacian_var:.2f}")
            return False

        # Exposure validation (histogram spread)
        hist = cv2.calcHist([gray], [0], None, [256], [0, 256])
        hist_norm = hist.ravel() / hist.sum()
        cumulative = np.cumsum(hist_norm)

        # Check for under/over exposure
        p5, p95 = np.searchsorted(cumulative, [0.05, 0.95])
        exposure_range = p95 - p5

        if exposure_range < self.config['min_histogram_range']:
            print(f"Poor exposure: range={exposure_range}")
            return False

        return True

    def _generate_exif_tags(self, sample_id):
        """Create EXIF metadata for traceability."""
        return {
            'Exif.Image.ImageDescription': f'Colony count sample {sample_id}',
            'Exif.Photo.UserComment': json.dumps({
                'sample_id': sample_id,
                'capture_time': datetime.now().isoformat(),
                'magnification': self.config['magnification'],
                'led_intensity_ma': self.config['led_current'],
            }),
        }

The automated quality validation in the capture pipeline serves two purposes: immediate feedback to operators when re-capture is needed, and long-term monitoring of imaging system health. By logging Laplacian variance and exposure metrics alongside each capture, we established baseline distributions that triggered maintenance alerts when metrics drifted beyond 2σ thresholds—enabling predictive maintenance of the imaging hardware before quality degradation became impactful.

Stage 2: Preprocessing Pipeline

Raw images from the capture system undergo a series of deterministic transformations designed to normalize input variability and enhance features relevant to colony detection. The preprocessing pipeline is deliberately architecture-agnostic—we've validated that the same preprocessing produces equivalent detection performance whether the downstream model is Mask R-CNN, YOLOv8, or a U-Net variant. This modularity has proven valuable during model iteration experiments.

Color Space Analysis and Selection

Standard RGB color encoding, while convenient for display, is suboptimal for segmentation tasks where illumination variance must be decoupled from chromatic content. After extensive experimentation with HSV, LAB, and YCrCb color spaces, we settled on CIE L*a*b* for the primary processing pipeline.

The L* channel (lightness) encodes perceived brightness independently of color information—the a* axis represents the green-red opponent channel, and b* represents blue-yellow. This separation enables us to apply contrast enhancement to L* without distorting color relationships that the species classifier relies upon. 

import cv2
import numpy as np
from typing import Tuple, Optional

class Preprocessor:
    """
    Image preprocessing pipeline optimized for colony detection.

    Implements adaptive normalization techniques robust to common
    imaging artifacts: uneven illumination, sensor noise, and
    color temperature variations.
    """

    def __init__(self, config: dict):
        self.target_size = config.get('target_size', (1024, 1024))
        self.clahe_clip = config.get('clahe_clip_limit', 2.0)
        self.clahe_grid = config.get('clahe_grid_size', (8, 8))
        self.denoise_h = config.get('denoise_h', 10)
        self.denoise_template_size = config.get('denoise_template_size', 7)

    def process(self, image_path: str) -> Tuple[np.ndarray, dict]:
        """
        Execute full preprocessing pipeline.

        Returns:
            processed_image: Normalized image ready for detection
            metadata: Dictionary of pipeline statistics for logging
        """
        metadata = {'original_shape': None, 'processing_steps': []}

        # Load image
        img = cv2.imread(image_path)
        if img is None:
            raise ValueError(f"Could not load image: {image_path}")

        metadata['original_shape'] = img.shape

        # Step 1: Petri dish boundary detection and cropping
        img, boundary_meta = self._extract_roi(img)
        metadata['processing_steps'].append(('roi_extraction', boundary_meta))

        # Step 2: Color space conversion and channel separation
        lab = cv2.cvtColor(img, cv2.COLOR_BGR2LAB)
        l_channel, a_channel, b_channel = cv2.split(lab)

        # Step 3: Adaptive contrast enhancement (CLAHE)
        clahe = cv2.createCLAHE(
            clipLimit=self.clahe_clip,
            tileGridSize=self.clahe_grid
        )
        l_enhanced = clahe.apply(l_channel)
        metadata['processing_steps'].append(('clahe', {
            'clip_limit': self.clahe_clip,
            'l_channel_mean_before': float(l_channel.mean()),
            'l_channel_mean_after': float(l_enhanced.mean())
        }))

        # Step 4: Merge enhanced L with original a,b channels
        lab_enhanced = cv2.merge([l_enhanced, a_channel, b_channel])
        enhanced_rgb = cv2.cvtColor(lab_enhanced, cv2.COLOR_LAB2BGR)

        # Step 5: Denoising (edge-preserving)
        denoised = cv2.fastNlMeansDenoisingColored(
            enhanced_rgb,
            None,
            h=self.denoise_h,
            hColor=self.denoise_h,
            templateWindowSize=self.denoise_template_size,
            searchWindowSize=21
        )
        metadata['processing_steps'].append(('denoising', {
            'h_param': self.denoise_h,
            'psnr_estimate': self._estimate_psnr(enhanced_rgb, denoised)
        }))

        # Step 6: Resize to model input dimensions
        resized = cv2.resize(denoised, self.target_size, interpolation=cv2.INTER_LINEAR)

        return resized, metadata

    def _extract_roi(self, img: np.ndarray) -> Tuple[np.ndarray, dict]:
        """
        Detect petri dish boundary and crop to region of interest.

        Uses Hough Circle Transform for robust detection under
        various illumination conditions.
        """
        gray = cv2.cvtColor(img, cv2.COLOR_BGR2GRAY)
        blurred = cv2.GaussianBlur(gray, (15, 15), 0)

        # Hough Circle detection
        circles = cv2.HoughCircles(
            blurred,
            cv2.HOUGH_GRADIENT,
            dp=1,
            minDist=gray.shape[0],  # Expect single circle
            param1=50,  # Canny edge threshold
            param2=30,  # Accumulator threshold
            minRadius=int(min(gray.shape) * 0.35),
            maxRadius=int(min(gray.shape) * 0.48)
        )

        if circles is not None:
            circles = np.round(circles[0, :]).astype("int")
            x, y, r = circles[0]

            # Crop to circle with margin
            margin = int(r * 0.02)
            x1, y1 = max(0, x - r - margin), max(0, y - r - margin)
            x2, y2 = min(img.shape[1], x + r + margin), min(img.shape[0], y + r + margin)

            cropped = img[y1:y2, x1:x2]
            meta = {'center': (int(x), int(y)), 'radius': int(r), 'method': 'hough'}

            return cropped, meta

        # Fallback: return full image if circle not detected
        return img, {'method': 'full_frame_fallback'}

    def _estimate_psnr(self, original: np.ndarray, processed: np.ndarray) -> float:
        """Estimate PSNR between original and processed images."""
        mse = np.mean((original.astype(float) - processed.astype(float)) ** 2)
        if mse == 0:
            return float('inf')
        max_pixel = 255.0
        return float(20 * np.log10(max_pixel / np.sqrt(mse)))

Why CLAHE Over Global Histogram Equalization?

Global histogram equalization (GHE) applies a single intensity transformation across the entire image based on the aggregate histogram. While this maximizes global contrast, it produces artifacts in regions with different local statistics—amplifying noise in dark regions and compressing useful information in bright regions.

Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE) addresses these limitations by computing histograms in localized windows (tiles) and applying clip limiting to prevent over-amplification of noise. The clip limit parameter controls the maximum height of any histogram bin—values exceeding the limit are redistributed to other bins, preventing the contrast blown-out regions that plague standard adaptive equalization.

CLAHE Clip Limit: β = (M × N) / (Nbins × clip_limit)

Where M×N is the tile size and Nbins is the number of histogram bins (typically 256). Our empirical validation found clip_limit=2.0 and tile_size=8×8 produced optimal results across 15 species with varying colony morphologies—from the diffuse edges of Aspergillus niger to the well-defined circular colonies of E. coli.

Stage 3: Detection Pipeline with Mask R-CNN

Colony detection presents unique challenges not addressed by standard object detection pipelines. Colonies frequently touch or overlap, particularly at higher densities—bounding box approaches struggle to separate individual instances. Instance segmentation, which predicts both an object class and a pixel-wise mask for each instance, is the natural fit for this problem.

Architecture Selection Rationale

We evaluated three architecture families for the detection head: Mask R-CNN, YOLOv8-seg (segmentation variant), and Mask2Former. Each has distinct trade-offs:

Mask R-CNN (Selected)

  • Two-stage detector with RoIAlign
  • Best mask quality for small objects
  • Slower inference (~45ms/image)
  • Mature ecosystem (Detectron2)

YOLOv8-seg

  • Single-stage, real-time capable
  • Faster inference (~15ms/image)
  • Poorer mask quality at boundaries
  • Struggles with touching instances

Mask R-CNN's RoIAlign operation preserves spatial precision by avoiding the quantization artifacts of earlier RoIPool. For colony detection, where accurate boundary delineation is critical for distinguishing touching colonies, this precision advantage outweighed the speed penalty—particularly given our throughput requirement of 500 samples/day, which Mask R-CNN comfortably satisfies.

Backbone Selection: ResNeXt-101 FPN

The backbone network extracts feature maps at multiple scales. For colony detection, we needed robust feature extraction at two extremes: very small colonies (< 0.5mm diameter, ~15 pixels) and larger fungal colonies spanning several centimeters. The Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) architecture addresses this by creating a multi-scale feature hierarchy—the high-resolution, low-semantic P2 level captures fine detail for small colonies while P5 captures context for larger structures.

We selected ResNeXt-101 over standard ResNet-101 for its improved accuracy/computation trade-off. ResNeXt introduces "cardinality"—parallel paths within each block that learn complementary features. With 64 groups (cardinality=64), ResNeXt-101 achieves comparable accuracy to ResNet-200 while using significantly fewer FLOPs. 

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
from detectron2.config import get_cfg
from detectron2.engine import DefaultPredictor, default_setup
from detectron2.modeling import build_model
from detectron2.checkpoint import DetectionCheckpointer
import numpy as np
from typing import Dict, List, Tuple

class ColonyDetector:
    """
    Mask R-CNN based colony detection and classification.

    Wraps Detectron2's DefaultPredictor with domain-specific
    post-processing and confidence calibration.
    """

    def __init__(self, config_path: str, weights_path: str, num_classes: int = 15):
        self.cfg = self._setup_config(config_path, weights_path, num_classes)
        self.predictor = DefaultPredictor(self.cfg)
        self.class_names = self._load_class_names()

        # Species-specific confidence thresholds (calibrated on validation set)
        self.confidence_thresholds = {
            0: 0.85,  # E. coli - high precision required
            1: 0.80,  # S. aureus
            2: 0.75,  # P. aeruginosa
            3: 0.80,  # B. subtilis
            4: 0.70,  # C. albicans (fungal, more variable morphology)
            # ... thresholds for remaining 10 classes
        }

    def _setup_config(self, config_path: str, weights_path: str, num_classes: int):
        """Configure model for inference."""
        cfg = get_cfg()
        cfg.merge_from_file(config_path)

        # Model weights
        cfg.MODEL.WEIGHTS = weights_path
        cfg.MODEL.ROI_HEADS.NUM_CLASSES = num_classes

        # Inference settings
        cfg.MODEL.ROI_HEADS.SCORE_THRESH_TEST = 0.5  # Initial threshold
        cfg.MODEL.ROI_HEADS.NMS_THRESH_TEST = 0.3

        # Input preprocessing
        cfg.INPUT.FORMAT = "BGR"
        cfg.INPUT.MIN_SIZE_TEST = 1024
        cfg.INPUT.MAX_SIZE_TEST = 1333

        return cfg

    def detect(self, image: np.ndarray) -> Dict[str, np.ndarray]:
        """
        Run detection pipeline on preprocessed image.

        Args:
            image: BGR image array, preprocessed and resized

        Returns:
            Dictionary containing boxes, masks, scores, and class predictions
        """
        with torch.no_grad():
            outputs = self.predictor(image)

        instances = outputs["instances"].to("cpu")

        # Extract predictions
        boxes = instances.pred_boxes.tensor.numpy()
        masks = instances.pred_masks.numpy()
        scores = instances.scores.numpy()
        classes = instances.pred_classes.numpy()

        # Apply class-specific confidence thresholds
        keep_indices = self._filter_by_confidence(scores, classes)

        return {
            'boxes': boxes[keep_indices],
            'masks': masks[keep_indices],
            'scores': scores[keep_indices],
            'classes': classes[keep_indices],
            'class_names': [self.class_names[c] for c in classes[keep_indices]],
            'count': len(keep_indices)
        }

    def _filter_by_confidence(self, scores: np.ndarray, classes: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
        """Apply per-class confidence thresholds."""
        keep = []
        for i, (score, cls) in enumerate(zip(scores, classes)):
            threshold = self.confidence_thresholds.get(cls, 0.7)
            if score >= threshold:
                keep.append(i)
        return np.array(keep)

    def compute_colony_metrics(self, detections: Dict) -> List[Dict]:
        """
        Compute morphological metrics for each detected colony.

        Returns per-colony statistics: area, perimeter, circularity,
        and centroid position.
        """
        metrics = []
        for i, (mask, class_id, score) in enumerate(zip(
            detections['masks'],
            detections['classes'],
            detections['scores']
        )):
            # Convert binary mask to contours
            mask_uint8 = (mask * 255).astype(np.uint8)
            contours, _ = cv2.findContours(
                mask_uint8, cv2.RETR_EXTERNAL, cv2.CHAIN_APPROX_SIMPLE
            )

            if len(contours) == 0:
                continue

            # Take largest contour (handles minor mask fragmentation)
            contour = max(contours, key=cv2.contourArea)

            area = cv2.contourArea(contour)
            perimeter = cv2.arcLength(contour, True)

            # Circularity: 4π × area / perimeter² (1.0 for perfect circle)
            circularity = (4 * np.pi * area) / (perimeter ** 2) if perimeter > 0 else 0

            # Centroid
            M = cv2.moments(contour)
            cx = M['m10'] / M['m00'] if M['m00'] > 0 else 0
            cy = M['m01'] / M['m00'] if M['m00'] > 0 else 0

            metrics.append({
                'colony_id': i,
                'species': self.class_names[class_id],
                'confidence': float(score),
                'area_pixels': float(area),
                'perimeter_pixels': float(perimeter),
                'circularity': float(circularity),
                'centroid': (float(cx), float(cy)),
                'bbox': detections['boxes'][i].tolist()
            })

        return metrics

Training Data and Annotation Strategy

Model performance is fundamentally constrained by annotation quality. We initially estimated needing 500 annotated plates per species for robust training—a total of 7,500 plates. This was neither feasible (each plate requires 15-45 minutes of expert annotation) nor necessary. Through active learning, we achieved target accuracy with only 2,400 annotated plates.

The annotation protocol evolved through three iterations:

  • Iteration 1: Bounding boxes only. Quick to annotate (~5 min/plate) but insufficient for distinguishing touching colonies. Model achieved 72% detection accuracy.
  • Iteration 2: Polygon annotations around colony boundaries. Better separation (~20 min/plate) but annotators struggled with diffuse fungal edges. Detection accuracy improved to 84%.
  • Iteration 3: Instance segmentation masks with species labels. Annotators traced the "visible boundary" including irregular edges. Final accuracy: 94% with active learning iteration.
Key Insight: Annotation consistency matters more than annotation volume. We achieved better results with 2,400 consistently annotated plates than our prototype with 4,000 inconsistently annotated plates. Inter-annotator agreement (IoU) improved from 0.71 to 0.89 after establishing clear annotation guidelines with visual examples.

Data Augmentation Strategy

Microbiological images present unique augmentation challenges—random flips and rotations that work well for natural images can create biologically implausible samples. We implemented domain-aware augmentations: 

from detectron2.data import transforms as T
from detectron2.data import DatasetMapper

def build_augmentation_mapper(cfg, is_train=True):
    """
    Custom augmentation pipeline for colony detection.

    Designed to simulate real-world imaging variability while
    preserving biological plausibility.
    """
    if is_train:
        augmentations = [
            # Geometric: only horizontal flip (plates are rotationally symmetric)
            T.RandomFlip(prob=0.5, horizontal=True, vertical=False),

            # Scale augmentation: simulate working distance variation
            T.ResizeShortestEdge(
                short_edge_length=[800, 900, 1000, 1100, 1200],
                max_size=1333,
                sample_style='choice'
            ),

            # Color augmentations: simulate illumination variation
            T.RandomBrightness(0.8, 1.2),
            T.RandomContrast(0.8, 1.2),
            T.RandomSaturation(0.8, 1.2),

            # Simulate sensor noise
            T.RandomLighting(scale=0.1),
        ]
    else:
        augmentations = [
            T.ResizeShortestEdge(short_edge_length=1024, max_size=1333),
        ]

    return DatasetMapper(cfg, is_train=is_train, augmentations=augmentations)

Stage 4: Growth Rate Calculation

Beyond instantaneous colony counting, the system tracks colony growth over time to compute exponential growth rates—a key metric for quality control of culture media. Growth rate calculation enables predictive alerts when growth deviates from expected patterns, potentially indicating media formulation issues or contamination.

Exponential Growth Model

Bacterial colonies in ideal conditions follow exponential growth:

N(t) = N₀ × e^(kt)

Where N(t) is the population at time t, N₀ is the initial population, and k is the growth rate constant (hours⁻¹). The doubling time (time for population to double) is derived as:

t_d = ln(2) / k ≈ 0.693 / k

We adapted this model for colony counts on plates, recognizing that the detected count represents a sample of the viable population. The growth rate we compute reflects the observable colony formation rate, which correlates with—but is not identical to—the underlying cell division rate. 

import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import curve_fit
from typing import Dict, Optional
import warnings

class GrowthRateAnalyzer:
    """
    Compute microbial growth rates from time-series colony counts.

    Implements exponential growth fitting with confidence intervals
    and anomaly detection for quality control applications.
    """

    def __init__(self, species_growth_bounds: Dict[str, tuple]):
        """
        Args:
            species_growth_bounds: Expected growth rate ranges (k_min, k_max)
                                   for each species, derived from historical data
        """
        self.species_bounds = species_growth_bounds

    def fit_growth_curve(
        self,
        time_points: np.ndarray,
        colony_counts: np.ndarray,
        species: str
    ) -> Dict:
        """
        Fit exponential growth model to time-series counts.
        """
        def exponential_growth(t, N0, k):
            return N0 * np.exp(np.clip(k * t, -50, 50))

        # Initial parameter estimates
        N0_init = colony_counts[0] if colony_counts[0] > 0 else 1

        if colony_counts[-1] > colony_counts[0] and colony_counts[0] > 0:
            k_init = np.log(colony_counts[-1] / colony_counts[0]) / (time_points[-1] - time_points[0])
        else:
            k_init = 0.1

        try:
            popt, pcov = curve_fit(
                exponential_growth,
                time_points,
                colony_counts,
                p0=[N0_init, k_init],
                bounds=([0, 0.001], [10000, 2.0]),
                maxfev=5000
            )
            perr = np.sqrt(np.diag(pcov))
            N0, k = popt
            N0_err, k_err = perr

        except (RuntimeError, ValueError) as e:
            warnings.warn(f"Growth curve fitting failed: {e}")
            return {
                'success': False,
                'error': str(e),
                'doubling_time_hours': np.log(2) / k_init if k_init > 0 else float('inf')
            }

        predicted = exponential_growth(time_points, N0, k)
        r_squared = self._compute_r_squared(colony_counts, predicted)
        doubling_time = np.log(2) / k if k > 0 else float('inf')

        expected_bounds = self.species_bounds.get(species, (0.05, 1.0))
        growth_anomaly = not (expected_bounds[0] <= k <= expected_bounds[1])

        return {
            'success': True,
            'N0': float(N0),
            'growth_rate': float(k),
            'growth_rate_std_error': float(k_err),
            'doubling_time_hours': float(doubling_time),
            'r_squared': float(r_squared),
            'growth_anomaly': growth_anomaly,
            'expected_range': expected_bounds
        }

    def _compute_r_squared(self, observed: np.ndarray, predicted: np.ndarray) -> float:
        ss_res = np.sum((observed - predicted) ** 2)
        ss_tot = np.sum((observed - np.mean(observed)) ** 2)
        return 1 - (ss_res / ss_tot) if ss_tot > 0 else 0.0

Edge Computing Layer

Each laboratory station operates a Raspberry Pi 4 (4GB) as the edge compute node. The Pi handles:

  • Image Acquisition: Camera control, illumination sequencing, capture triggering
  • Preprocessing: CLAHE enhancement, denoising, ROI extraction (CPU-bound operations)
  • Compression: JPEG encoding at quality 95 for bandwidth-efficient upload
  • Local Caching: 24-hour rolling cache for offline resilience
  • LIMS Integration: Direct database writes for sample metadata

The edge layer performs all latency-sensitive operations locally—capture, preprocessing, and LIMS updates complete in under 3 seconds. Only the detection inference requires cloud round-trip, adding 400-600ms latency for a total end-to-end processing time of approximately 3.5 seconds per sample.

Cloud Inference Layer

Detection inference runs on AWS SageMaker with a ml.g4dn.xlarge instance (NVIDIA T4 GPU, 4 vCPU, 16GB GPU memory). The T4's 16GB VRAM comfortably accommodates the ResNeXt-101 FPN backbone with batch size 4 for efficient throughput. Model serving uses the NVIDIA Triton Inference Server with ONNX export for optimized inference:

  • Model Format: ONNX (exported from PyTorch via torch.onnx.export)
  • Optimization: TensorRT acceleration for 2.3x speedup over native PyTorch
  • Batching: Dynamic batching with 100ms timeout, max batch size 8
  • Quantization: FP16 precision (no measurable accuracy degradation, 1.8x speedup)
# SageMaker inference entry point
import json
import base64
import numpy as np
from io import BytesIO
from PIL import Image
import detectron2
from detectron2.config import get_cfg
from detectron2.engine import DefaultPredictor

def model_fn(model_dir):
    """Load model for inference."""
    cfg = get_cfg()
    cfg.merge_from_file(f"{model_dir}/config.yaml")
    cfg.MODEL.WEIGHTS = f"{model_dir}/model_final.pth"
    predictor = DefaultPredictor(cfg)
    return {"predictor": predictor, "cfg": cfg}

def input_fn(request_body, request_content_type):
    """Parse input data."""
    if request_content_type == "application/json":
        data = json.loads(request_body)
        image_bytes = base64.b64decode(data["image"])
        image = np.array(Image.open(BytesIO(image_bytes)).convert("RGB"))
        return image
    raise ValueError(f"Unsupported content type: {request_content_type}")

def predict_fn(input_data, model):
    """Run inference."""
    predictor = model["predictor"]
    outputs = predictor(input_data)

    instances = outputs["instances"].to("cpu")
    return {
        "boxes": instances.pred_boxes.tensor.numpy().tolist(),
        "scores": instances.scores.numpy().tolist(),
        "classes": instances.pred_classes.numpy().tolist(),
        "masks": instances.pred_masks.numpy().tolist() if instances.has("pred_masks") else None,
        "count": len(instances)
    }

def output_fn(prediction, content_type):
    """Format response."""
    return json.dumps(prediction), content_type

Technical Learnings

Lesson 1 - Annotation Quality Over Quantity: We initially pursued volume, annotating as many plates as possible. This was counterproductive. Switching to an active learning approach where we annotated only high-uncertainty samples improved model accuracy by 6% while reducing annotation effort by 40%.
Lesson 2 - Edge Case Discovery Never Ends: Even after deployment, we encountered novel edge cases—unusual colony morphologies, media formulations we hadn't tested, lighting variations from seasonal changes. Building a feedback loop where technicians could flag problematic samples for review dramatically accelerated edge case discovery and model improvement.
Lesson 3 - Confidence Calibration is Critical: Raw model confidence scores were poorly calibrated—scores of 0.9 often corresponded to only 70% precision. Implementing temperature scaling on a held-out validation set improved calibration from ECE 0.18 to 0.06, making confidence thresholds meaningful for downstream decision-making.

When to Use This Approach

✅ Ideal Conditions

  • Consistent Imaging Pipeline: Fixed camera, lighting, and sample positioning enable strong model generalization
  • Bounded Organism Set: Classification works best with known, finite target classes (retraining required for novel species)
  • Integration Requirements: When LIMS/MES integration is critical, software solutions offer flexibility hardware cannot
  • Volume-driven ROI: At >200 samples/day, labor savings justify development investment within 18-24 months
  • Time-series Analysis: When growth rate tracking adds value beyond instantaneous counts
  • Multi-site Deployment: Centralized model management reduces per-site maintenance burden

⛔ Limitations & Constraints

  • Novel Organisms: Species not in training data require annotation effort and model retraining
  • Variable Imaging Conditions: Significant lighting/positioning variation degrades accuracy
  • Regulatory Certification: Medical device regulations may require certified instruments
  • Real-time Requirements: 3-4 second latency unsuitable for time-critical workflows
  • High Confluence Plates: Colony overlap >70% reduces detection accuracy significantly
  • Offline Requirements: Cloud dependency requires reliable internet connectivity

Alternative Approaches

For scenarios where this approach isn't suitable, consider these alternatives:

  • YOLOv8-seg for Real-time: If sub-second latency is required, YOLOv8's single-stage architecture reduces inference to 15-20ms at the cost of mask quality—acceptable for counting applications where precise boundary delineation isn't critical.
  • On-premise GPU Deployment: If cloud dependency is unacceptable, local GPU servers (RTX 3060 class) can run the same model pipeline with zero external network dependencies.
  • Semi-supervised Learning: For scenarios with limited annotation budget, pseudo-labeling on unlabeled data can bootstrap training with 60-70% annotation effort reduction.
